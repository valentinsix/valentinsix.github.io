# Writing style notes

A personal reference for the tone and voice I want in blog posts. This is not about structure or formatting (headings, bullet lists, etc.), just how the writing should *sound*.

Reference posts, for voice and tone only. Structurally they both lean on bullet points more than I want:

- [A Pragmatic Vision for Interpretability](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/StENzDcD3kpfGJssR/a-pragmatic-vision-for-interpretability)
- [Towards Developmental Interpretability](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/TjaeCWvLZtEDAS5Ex/towards-developmental-interpretability)

## Adopt

**Vary sentence length on purpose.** Let short, blunt sentences land after a longer, exploratory one. A long sentence stringing together clauses and parentheticals can carry nuance; the short sentence after it delivers the point. Don't let every sentence be medium-length: that's what reads as flat or generated.

**Hedge honestly, then commit.** Use "I think," "my understanding is," "it seems like" when the claim is genuinely uncertain, not as a reflex on every sentence. Once hedged, state the claim plainly rather than burying it in qualifiers. The goal is calibration, not caution.

**Write in first person, plainly.** "I've been reading about X" and "I wanted to write down what I understood" are fine. Own the perspective instead of writing around it in passive voice.

**Use rhetorical questions to open a section**, not to pad one. "Why does this matter?" earns its place if the next sentence actually answers it.

**Build from a concrete example before naming the abstraction.** Show the specific case, then generalize, not the reverse. This is the strongest shared trait of both reference posts: they earn the abstraction instead of asserting it.

**Let dry, understated humor slip in occasionally**, through a parenthetical aside, an "unfortunately not," a mildly self-deprecating aside about being wrong before. It should read as a side effect of honesty, not a bit.

**Use parentheticals for a genuine aside**, not for information that belongs in the main sentence. If removing the parenthetical would lose something, keep it; if it's just hedge-padding, cut it.

**Be explicit about what you don't know.** A section like "where this breaks down" or "reasons I might be wrong" is more convincing than an unqualified pitch. Confidence should track the actual state of understanding, not project more certainty than exists.

**Assume the reader knows the field, lightly.** Use the field's jargon (e.g. RLCT, singularity, phase transition) without stopping to define every term from scratch — but define the load-bearing ones once, briefly, the first time they appear.

## Avoid

**Don't hedge every sentence.** "It seems like," "I think," "arguably" stacked on consecutive sentences reads as evasive, not humble. Hedge the claim that needs it, commit to the ones that don't.

**Don't open with throat-clearing.** Skip "In this post, I will discuss...". Start with the actual thought or example.

**Don't over-explain a term right after using it in a way that already made it clear.** Trust the reader once.

**Don't reach for a bullet list to avoid writing a transition.** A list of three short parallel facts is fine; a list where each item is really a paragraph in disguise means the prose isn't doing its job. Prefer connected prose for reasoning and save lists for genuinely enumerable things.

**Don't flatten sentence rhythm.** A string of same-length, same-structure sentences is the biggest tell of stiff or AI-generated prose. Read a paragraph out loud. If it has no variation in cadence, rewrite it.

**Don't oversell confidence.** Avoid words like "clearly," "obviously," "simply" when the point isn't actually simple or obvious to the reader. They're usually there to cover for a gap in the explanation, not to help it.

**Don't use humor as a device.** No forced jokes, no emoji, no "lol". If something's funny, it should come from the honesty of the observation, not from trying to be funny.

**Don't use em dashes to splice sentences together.** The "clause — clause — clause" pattern is one of the most overused LLM tics. Use a period, a comma, a colon, or a parenthesis instead, whichever actually fits the relationship between the two ideas. An em dash is fine on rare occasion for a genuine interruption, not as the default connector.

## Quick self-check before publishing

- Read it out loud. Does the rhythm vary, or does every sentence sound the same length?
- Find every hedge word. Cut the ones that aren't doing real epistemic work.
- Is there at least one place where I say what I don't know or where I could be wrong?
- Did I show an example before naming the abstract idea, or did I do it backwards?
- Cut the throat-clearing opening line, if there is one.
